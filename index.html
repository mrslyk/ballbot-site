The v1 mission

One job, done reliably.

Every ball machine, drill, and lesson ends the same way — dozens of balls scattered across the court and someone crouched over a hopper. Ballbot v1 does exactly one thing well: see the balls → drive to them → lift them into a hopper → return to the dock. Everything fancier is deferred until that core loop is rock-solid — because it's the platform every future feature bolts onto.

How it works

Four steps, on repeat.

An overhead camera gives Ballbot a bird's-eye map of the whole court, so it always knows where every ball is and where it is. Then it just works the list.

The pickup mechanism

Like a bowling alley — shrunk to tennis scale.

A tennis ball machine already grips a ball between two counter-rotating rubber wheels to launch it. Run those wheels the other way and they become an intake that lifts the ball instead. It's the bowling-alley ball-return trick, and because ball machines prove the hardware at this exact scale, it's low-risk and simple.

  • A low scoop lip rides ~2–3 mm off the court, so a resting ball rolls up onto it instead of being pushed away.
  • A funnel throat narrows to the wheel gap, so aim never has to be perfect.
  • Two wheels set ~50–55 mm apart squeeze the 65 mm ball and fling it up a short chute — one motor, one belt.
  • Fallback we'll bench-test: a single brush roller, even simpler, and we keep whichever grips better.
Fence & corner balls

No ball left behind.

The hard part of any front-mouth collector is that it can't push its nose flush into a 90° corner, and a ball hugging the fence sits just outside the intake path. Ballbot borrows the fix from street sweepers and robot vacuums: a rotating side-sweeper brush at the front corner that reaches past the body.

  • When the overhead camera sees balls near the fence, Ballbot runs a wall-follow routine.
  • The angled gutter brush flicks the ball inward, off the fence and into the intake's path.
  • For a true apex, it approaches diagonally and rakes the ball out along one fence line first.
  • Just one extra small motor — a thoroughly proven mechanism.
The brain

Finding balls is the easy part.

Optic yellow is the most distinctive color on a tennis court — nothing else shares it — so Ballbot needs no heavy machine learning. A clean color-vision pipeline does the whole job.

Electronics & compute

Two brains, one nervous system.

Ballbot splits its computing the way most robots do: a Linux single-board computer handles the heavy perception and planning, while a small microcontroller runs the hard-real-time motor-and-sensor loop — so vision hiccups can never stall a motor command or the e-stop. The overhead camera stays dumb in v1, just streaming frames to the onboard computer over Wi-Fi.
