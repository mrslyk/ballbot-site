Ballbot — Version 1 Design

A solar-assisted robot that finds tennis balls on a court, sweeps them into a hopper, and returns to its dock.

Prepared for Tim Parsa & Kevin Eisenbacher · July 17, 2026 · v1 concept — scoping draft

The whole game in version 1 is one loop done reliably: see the balls → drive to them → lift them into a hopper → return to the dock. Kevin's longer wishlist — serving gun, court squeegee, floor conditioner, debris blower, court-to-court travel — stays the north star but is deferred. This draft focuses on the two things you flagged: the simplest possible pickup mechanism, and how it reaches balls against the fence and in the corners. Schematics below.

1. The pickup mechanism — yes, like a bowling alley

Your instinct is exactly right, and it's the same trick a tennis ball machine already uses in reverse. A ball machine launches a ball by pinching it between two counter-rotating rubber wheels. Run those wheels the other direction and they become an intake: the ball gets grabbed at the bottom and thrown up a short chute into the hopper. It's the bowling-alley ball-return principle, shrunk to tennis-ball scale — and because ball machines already prove the hardware works at this size, it's low-risk.

How a ball gets from the ground into the hopper, front to back:

  • A low scoop lip at the very front, riding ~2–3 mm off the court on a smooth skid. As the robot drives forward, a resting ball rolls up onto the lip instead of being pushed away.
  • A funnel throat that narrows from a wide mouth down to the wheel gap, so the ball doesn't need to be perfectly centered — anything in the mouth gets steered into the nip.
  • Two counter-rotating wheels (~70 mm foam-over-rubber), set a bit closer than a ball is wide (~50–55 mm gap). The ball (65 mm) is squeezed to ~52 mm, the wheels grip it, and their upward-moving faces fling it up the chute.
  • A short curved chute carries the ball up and over into the hopper. One drive belt can spin both wheels off a single small motor.
Why this over a scoop or a plow: a passive scoop or ramp just shoves a ball ahead of it — the ball rolls away. Anything that reliably moves a ball up and over a lip needs to grip it, and spinning wheels grip. Two wheels and one motor is about as simple as a reliable lift gets.

Simpler fallback: one brush roller

If we want to prototype even cheaper first, a single spinning bristle roller (like a powered push-sweeper or driving-range collector) can flick balls up the ramp with one motor and no precise gap to tune. It grips a fast, bouncy tennis ball a little less surely than the twin wheels, so the plan is to bench-test both cheaply and keep whichever proves more reliable. Lead design = twin wheels; brush = backup.

2. Reaching balls at the fence and in the corners

This is the real weakness of any front-mouth collector: it can't push its nose flush into a 90° corner, and a ball hugging the fence sits just outside the intake's path. The fix is borrowed from street sweepers and robot vacuums: a rotating side-sweeper brush mounted at the front corner of the robot, sticking out past the body edge.

  • The robot runs a "wall-follow" routine along the fence when the overhead camera sees balls near it.
  • The angled, vertical-axis gutter brush reaches into the corner and against the fence, and flicks the ball inward — away from the fence, into the robot's forward path where the twin-wheel intake grabs it.
  • It costs one extra small motor and is a thoroughly proven mechanism (every Roomba and street sweeper uses it).

For a true corner, the robot approaches diagonally so the brush can rake the ball out along one fence line first, then the intake collects it on the follow-through. Balls that are genuinely pinned in the apex are the hardest 1–2% — the brush handles nearly all of them; the rest we accept as a known limit for v1.

4. The camera + "find all the balls" program

Detection is the easy part, because optic yellow is the most distinctive color on a court — nothing else shares it. No heavy machine learning needed for v1; a straightforward color-vision pipeline does it:

  • Overhead camera on a net post or fence, looking down at the whole court, is the backbone — it sees every ball at once and tracks the robot too.
  • Color mask: filter each frame for the optic-yellow band (HSV threshold) → clean blobs → a list of ball positions in court coordinates.
  • Route: a simple nearest-ball-next plan (greedy, with corner balls flagged for the brush routine) orders the pickups to minimize driving.
  • Drive & collect: robot drives to each ball; the forward onboard camera does the final centering into the intake mouth.
  • Fallback: if a camera is blocked, the robot can still mow the whole rectangle in a back-and-forth pattern like a robot vacuum and sweep everything up.

5. Electronics & compute architecture

Ballbot splits its computing the standard robotics way: a Linux single-board computer (the "brain") handles the heavy, non-real-time perception and planning, while a small microcontroller runs the hard-real-time motor-and-sensor loop. That split matters — it means a momentary hiccup in the vision pipeline can never stall a motor command or delay the e-stop. In v1 the overhead camera stays dumb: it just streams frames over Wi-Fi to the onboard computer, which does all the processing.

The compute split, concretely

  • Onboard computer (the brain): a Linux SBC — a Raspberry Pi 5 is ample for the classic OpenCV color-vision pipeline; step up to a Jetson Orin Nano only if we later add a learned detector for tricky lighting or heavy occlusion. Runs frame capture, the optic-yellow mask, homography to court coordinates, the route planner, and the motion controller.
  • Real-time microcontroller: a Teensy 4.1 or ESP32 handling motor PWM, quadrature encoder reads, sensor polling, and a hardware e-stop. Talks to the SBC over USB serial, taking velocity commands and returning odometry.
  • Overhead camera: kept dumb in v1 — streams frames to the SBC over Wi-Fi. A later version could push detection onto a small edge board at the camera to cut bandwidth.
  • Vision stack: capture → HSV optic-yellow mask → contour/blob detection → homography (pixel→court, calibrated once off the four court corners) → robot pose from an overhead marker fused with onboard odometry/IMU → greedy nearest-ball route with corner flags → velocity commands.

6. Hopper — sizing to standard capacities

Rather than invent a size, we anchor to what teaching hoppers and carts already come in. Common commercial capacities: Gamma baskets ship in 50, 55, 75, 80, 90, 110, and 140; folding rolling teaching carts are typically 150. A standard lesson/drill basket is 50–75 balls, and a coach's cart is ~150.

OptionCapacityFits v1?
Small basket50–55Too many dump trips on a full court
Mid basket (recommended)75–110Best balance of size vs. dump frequency
Coach cart140–150Great capacity, but a taller/heavier robot

Recommendation: design the hopper cavity around a standard 75–110 ball basket — enough to clear a typical loose-ball session in one or two trips without making the robot tall and tippy. Keeping it to an off-the-shelf basket size also means the removable bin can literally be a stock hopper we drop in and lift out to empty.

8. Recommended v1 baseline

Fastest path to a demoable robot: twin-wheel bowling-alley intake with a funnel and scoop lip; a front-corner side brush plus wall-follow routine for fence and corner balls; an overhead camera + simple yellow-blob detector as the brain, with a forward camera for final homing; a standard 75–110 ball hopper as a drop-in bin; half-court jobs, run between sessions, and dock-primary power with a token solar panel. Smallest machine that convincingly does the core loop — and the platform every later feature bolts onto.
